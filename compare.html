Choosing a Course Scheduling Tool for Your Department

If you're a department chair or scheduler deciding between spreadsheets, a dedicated tool, or an enterprise platform, here's a practical comparison. The right choice depends on whether you need institutional-level optimization or department-level clarity and on who's allowed to make the decision.

Three tiers of course scheduling tools

Department-level course scheduling in higher education falls into three broad categories. Each serves a different role, requires a different level of institutional commitment, and is adopted by a different decision-maker.

Spreadsheets

Excel & Google Sheets

Universally available and infinitely flexible. No approval needed. But they don't understand scheduling — no conflict detection, no synchronized views, no purpose-built visualization. The person who builds the formulas becomes a single point of failure.

Department Tool

TermPoint

A desktop application purpose-built for department-level timetabling. Visual scheduling grid, multi-dimensional filtering, conflict detection, workload tracking. Runs locally, no IT involvement, adopted by a single department. $39.95 CAD/year.

Enterprise Suites

Coursedog, Ad Astra, Infosilem, CourseLeaf CLSS, etc.

Institutional platforms with SIS integration, optimization algorithms, cross-campus coordination, and analytics. Powerful, but require top-down deployment, IT involvement, and institutional contracts. Adopted by the registrar or provost, not by a department. Typically very expensive.

Where TermPoint fits

TermPoint sits between spreadsheets and enterprise scheduling suites. It is a simple visualization and record-keeping tool for department-level course scheduling — purpose-built for the person who actually shapes the timetable. It does not optimize and it does not integrate with your SIS. What it does do is give a department chair or scheduler a clear, filterable, conflict-aware view of their schedule, so they can make good decisions and hand off a clean result to the registrar. It can be adopted by a single department without institutional buy-in, IT provisioning, or budget approval beyond a department credit card.

TermPoint vs. Excel and Google Sheets

The most common starting point for department scheduling

Most department chairs build their course schedule in a spreadsheet because there's no realistic alternative. Enterprise platforms require institutional deployment; spreadsheets are already on every machine. The problem isn't that spreadsheets are bad tools — it's that scheduling isn't what they were built for. Every department chair who has inherited a scheduling spreadsheet from a predecessor knows the fragility: macros that break, tabs that drift out of sync, conflicts that hide in plain sight.

Some resourceful chairs go further and build custom VBA macro systems in Excel — complete with visual grids, conflict highlighting, and automated formatting. With AI coding assistants now widely available, it's easier than ever to get a working prototype. This approach can work for the person who can build it (and has the time and patience to do so). More challenging is everything that comes after: making it reliable across edge cases, maintaining it when Excel updates break something, handing it off when the chair rotates out of the role, and supporting a colleague who isn't comfortable debugging VBA. The gap between a working prototype and a tool your successor can depend on is where most homebrew scheduling spreadsheets eventually stall. TermPoint exists in part because its senior developer walked that exact path — building a VBA scheduling tool, watching it spread to other departments, and recognizing the limits of what a macro-driven spreadsheet could sustain.

Spreadsheets

Spreadsheets are flexible, and that can be useful. But the cost of that flexibility is that they're typically unstructured. They have no awareness of scheduling concepts — a "room" is just a cell value. Conflicts are invisible until someone notices. Multiple-tab coordination breaks down at scale. Custom VBA macros can close some of these gaps, but reliable ones are labor intensive to build, even with AI help. And they create a new dependency: the person who wrote the macros.

TermPoint

In contrast, TermPoint understands rooms, instructors, time slots, and sections in terms of the role they play in the real-world. It's built from the ground up to allow you to see their interaction clearly. Filter the entire schedule by any dimension instantly. Change a section's time and every view updates. Conflicts surface the moment they're created, not after the schedule is submitted.

TermPoint vs. Coursedog

Cloud-based institutional scheduling and curriculum platform

Coursedog is an integrated academic operations platform that unifies course scheduling, curriculum management, catalog publishing, and demand analytics. It integrates with major SIS platforms (Ellucian Banner, PeopleSoft, Workday, Jenzabar) and is deployed at the institutional level. Coursedog is built for registrars and provosts managing scheduling across the entire institution.

Coursedog

Institutional platform with SIS sync, approval workflows, demand forecasting, and cross-campus collaboration. Requires institutional contract and IT implementation. Powerful, but adopted top-down — a single department cannot deploy it independently.

TermPoint

Department-level tool, no SIS integration, no institutional contract. A department chair can install it today and start building next term's schedule. Serves as the workspace where the schedule takes shape before being submitted to the registrar or entered into a system like Coursedog.

TermPoint vs. Ad Astra

AI-powered scheduling and academic planning for higher education

Ad Astra (aais.com) partners with over 550 institutions to provide course scheduling, event management, room optimization, and demand analytics. Its platform includes patented predictive analytics and cross-department planning tools. Like other enterprise platforms, Ad Astra requires institutional deployment, SIS integration, and significant training investment.

Ad Astra

Comprehensive institutional platform with AI-driven recommendations, registration monitoring, and multi-term planning. G2 reviewers note strong analytics but a steep learning curve. Pricing runs into the tens of thousands annually, with multi-year contracts.

TermPoint

No AI, no analytics, no learning curve. A single-purpose visualization tool that a department scheduler can open and use immediately. Serves departments that need to see and shape their schedule clearly, not institutions that need cross-campus optimization.

TermPoint vs. Infosilem

Constraint-based academic timetabling suite (Berger-Levrault)

Infosilem has served higher education scheduling for over 30 years, providing constraint-based timetable generation, room optimization, and exam scheduling. The platform uses algorithmic optimization to generate schedules that respect institutional rules and faculty constraints. It integrates with SIS platforms and supports both centralized and distributed scheduling workflows.

Infosilem

Optimization-first approach: define your constraints and the system generates a schedule. Powerful for institutions that can invest in configuration and rule definition. Requires IT infrastructure, SIS integration, and dedicated scheduling administrators to maintain.

TermPoint

Visualization-first approach: the scheduler sees the full picture and makes the decisions. No constraint engine, no algorithmic generation. TermPoint assumes the department scheduler knows their faculty, courses, and constraints better than any algorithm — they just need a clear view.

TermPoint vs. CourseLeaf CLSS

Section scheduler with Snapper visual grid

CourseLeaf CLSS is the closest purpose-built comparison to TermPoint's scheduling grid. Its Snapper feature provides a visual interface for placing sections into time slots with immediate conflict feedback, and it includes tools like the Heat Map for utilization analysis and the Framer for year-over-year comparison. CLSS is used at institutions ranging from Duke and Cal Poly to the University of Miami.

CourseLeaf CLSS

Institutional section scheduler with SIS integration (Banner, PeopleSoft), approval workflows, and validation rules defined by the registrar's office. Departments work within it, but deployment and configuration are managed centrally. The Snapper visual grid is a feature within the larger platform, not a standalone tool.

TermPoint

The visual grid and filtering are the entire product, not one feature among many. No SIS integration, no registrar-managed rules, no approval workflow. A standalone workspace for departments that want to build and refine their schedule independently before submitting to the central system.

TermPoint vs. Ellucian Banner schedule management

The SIS that many departments submit into, not schedule with

Ellucian Banner is the student information system used by hundreds of universities and colleges. Its schedule management module (SSASECT and related forms) is where course sections are officially recorded, but it is not a scheduling design tool. Most departments that use Banner as their SIS still build their schedule in Excel, then enter the finished product into Banner — or submit it to the registrar for entry.

Banner

The institutional system of record for student registration, not a schedule-building workspace. Data entry forms, not visual grids. No filtering by instructor or program across sections. The place the schedule goes after it's built, not where it's designed.

TermPoint

The workspace where the schedule is designed before it goes to Banner. TermPoint sits upstream of the SIS, giving departments a visual, filterable, conflict-aware environment for building the schedule that will eventually be entered into the system of record.

When to use what

Use a spreadsheet if your department schedules fewer than 20 sections, one person manages it entirely, and you have no recurring problems with conflicts or handoff errors. Spreadsheets are free and already on your machine.

Use TermPoint if your department schedules dozens or low hundreds of sections, you need to filter and visualize the schedule across multiple dimensions (instructor, room, program, level), you want conflict detection and workload tracking, and you need to do all of this without waiting for an institutional software deployment. TermPoint is the right tool when the department itself controls the scheduling workflow and hands off the result.

Use an enterprise platform if your institution needs cross-department optimization, demand forecasting, SIS-integrated workflows, or centralized control over the scheduling process. These platforms require institutional commitment, budget, and IT resources — but they solve problems (like institution-wide room optimization and enrollment-driven section planning) that no department-level tool can.

Frequently asked questions

What if our institution already has an enterprise scheduling system?

TermPoint is not an enterprise scheduling system, but can complement one. Many institutions have centralized platforms, but the day-to-day work of building a department schedule still happens in spreadsheets because the enterprise tool is either too complex for department-level use, or requires permissions the chair does not have. TermPoint fills that gap — it is a workspace for building and refining the schedule that will eventually be submitted to the central system. Note that TermPoint never touches the system of record, never becomes an alternate source of truth for anyone but its own user (who after-all is probably maintaining a source of truth in their spreadsheets.) What it changes is the quality of what arrives at the registrar's desk: requests that have already been checked against the department's own constraints, fewer rounds of revision, fewer collisions discovered late.

Is TermPoint a replacement for Coursedog, Ad Astra, or Infosilem?

No. TermPoint is not an enterprise scheduling platform and does not compete with institutional systems. Those platforms manage scheduling at the institutional level with SIS integration, optimization algorithms, and cross-campus coordination. TermPoint is a lightweight desktop tool that helps individual departments visualize and build their portion of the schedule before handing it off to the registrar or central system. The two categories serve different roles in the scheduling workflow.

Can a single department adopt TermPoint without institutional IT involvement?

Yes. TermPoint is a Windows desktop application available from the Microsoft Store. It installs like any other desktop program — no database server, no SIS integration, no IT provisioning required. A department chair or scheduler can download it, open it, and start building a schedule immediately. Data stays in a local file on the user's machine or a shared network folder.

How does TermPoint compare to building a course schedule in Excel?

Spreadsheets are flexible but unstructured. They don't flag conflicts, they require manual coordination across tabs, and they break when the person who built the formulas moves on. TermPoint provides a purpose-built scheduling grid with multi-dimensional filtering (by instructor, room, subject, level, time, tags), automatic conflict detection, synchronized views that update together, and workload tracking — while keeping the same ease of adoption that makes spreadsheets attractive in the first place.

Could I just build my own scheduling tool in Excel with VBA macros or AI-generated code?

You can, and some schedulers do, at least those with sufficient time to invest. The Claude AI will offer to build you one in a few minutes, which after some hours of conversation back and forth will produce something serviceable, if clunky. The challenge is to create reliable and easy to use tool that doesn't create more headaches and awkward work-arounds than came before. A custom VBA scheduling workbook needs to handle edge cases reliably, survive Excel version updates, and be usable by whichever colleague or successor inherits it. Most homebrew scheduling spreadsheets work well for the person who built them and become a maintenance burden for everyone after. TermPoint's head developer originally went down this exact road, building a VBA-based scheduling tool that spread organically across departments. He built TermPoint when the limits of that approach became clear.

Does TermPoint integrate with Banner, PeopleSoft, or other SIS platforms?

No. TermPoint is designed to sit upstream of the SIS, not replace it. Departments use TermPoint to shape and visualize their schedule, then hand the finished result to the registrar for entry into the institutional system of record. This is intentional: it means TermPoint works at any institution regardless of which SIS they use, and requires zero integration work. The same is true of the spreadsheet approach, but the disadvantage there is that the authoritative data is graphical: the weekly grid. The user still needs to make the tedious and error-prone graphical-to-text translation when migrating the schedle to an SIS. In TermPoint, the authoritative data is already textual in very much the same format as an SIS schedule edit page. Migration is much easier.

How much does TermPoint cost compared to enterprise scheduling software?

TermPoint is $39.95 CAD per year for a department license, with a free 30-day trial from the Microsoft Store. Enterprise scheduling platforms such as Ad Astra, Coursedog, and Infosilem typically cost tens of thousands of dollars annually, require institutional contracts, and involve significant implementation overhead.

Does TermPoint have an optimization engine or auto-scheduler?

No. TermPoint is a visualization and record-keeping tool, not an optimizer. It is built on the principle that department schedulers — who understand faculty preferences, pedagogical constraints, and program needs — make better scheduling decisions than an algorithm, provided they can see the information clearly. TermPoint gives them that clarity.

Not yet. TermPoint currently runs on Windows 10 and later. A macOS version is planned.
